What is a bank switch offer?

A bank switch offer is a high street bank's way of incentivising you with cash or other freebies to move your main bank account over to them.

This remains profitable for the banks because new customers generate additional revenue through deposits, credit products, and transactional fees. They make a profit even while giving you sometimes up to £310 worth of rewards.

There's no specific clause stating you need to switch your main bank account — which is why the smart move is to create a burner (dummy) account that exists solely for the purpose of switching.

Golden Rule

Lenders love seeing a long-standing bank account on your credit report. Never switch away from your oldest bank account.

Direct debits — what & how

Most bank switch offers require you to have active direct debits on the account you're switching from, because that signals it's a real, functioning current account. A direct debit is an automatic payment that allows a company to take money directly from your account on a regular basis — think gym memberships, phone bills, credit card repayments.

You could redirect existing direct debits to your dummy account — but if you'd prefer to keep your main account untouched, here are three easy ones to set up:

Moneybox

A savings app — top up your savings pot for as little as £2/week. This money goes directly into your own savings, so it's essentially free.

Effectively free

30p Newsletter

Pay as little as 30p via direct debit for a newsletter at 30pence.co.uk. You can also do 60p or 80p for multiple direct debits.

From 30p/month

PayPal

Top up your PayPal balance with as little as £10. Once the first payment is taken, the direct debit is active. Doesn't need to be recurring.

One-off payment

Important

Before initiating any bank switch offer that requires direct debits, ensure the first payments have already been taken out from the account. Direct debits need to be active — not just set up — to count.

The dummy account strategy

You should never switch your main bank account — this carries much worse credit score implications. Instead, create a dummy account that exists solely for the purpose of switching.

Chase Bank is the best option for this. You can create additional accounts in seconds — each gets a unique sort code and account number that you can use for switching.

Important Chase note

Always keep at least 2 accounts open within Chase. Switching away your only Chase account closes your entire Chase profile — and they won't let you reopen it. Switching additional Chase accounts does not cancel your debit card.

Simply tap 'Add +' in the Chase app to create a new account. This generates a fresh sort code and account number instantly. You can input the same debit card details into each switch offer too.

06

Credit score impact

Switching bank accounts, opening new accounts, and closing old ones does have an impact on your credit score. It's important to go in with eyes open.

From personal experience: I've completed 14 bank switch offers, 5 student account offers, opened 3 new credit products, and 20+ banking products over the past year — and my credit score is still higher than it was last year, because I've been building credit with credit builder cards alongside switching.

Scared of the impact? Stick to 1–2 bank switches per year. You're typically limited to one switch per UK banking group anyway, so there's no need to rush.

Avoid bank switching if you're…

Planning to apply for a mortgage within 2 years — the account activity can affect affordability assessments.

Trying to build your credit score for premium credit cards (like Amex) — do the switches after you've opened credit products.

Unable to accurately track accounts — you need to be organised enough to close or consolidate them once the incentive is paid.

07

Tax implications

There are no tax implications to bank switch offers for the recipient. This is confirmed by HMRC directly.

HMRC Manual CG13027

"In general, ordinary retail customers purchasing goods, investments or services at arm's length will not be liable to Income or Capital Gains Tax in respect of any commission, discounts or cashbacks received by them."

This also applies to freebies from switch offers — taste cards, hotel credit, rail cards, gift cards. They're all classed as incentive or cashback income and are therefore not taxable.

The one exception

You are potentially liable for tax if you refer someone and personally receive a bonus for that referral. For example, referring friends to Monzo — that income contributes towards your £1,000/year trading allowance.

For most people, this is nothing to worry about. If you sign up to Monzo using a friend's link, you (the referee) do not pay any tax.

When in doubt, check HMRC.gov.uk directly or consult a qualified accountant. This guide is for educational purposes only.
